I am a philosopher of artificial intelligence investigating the human use and misuse of mathematical and computational tools in knowledge production and decision-making. I focus particularly on emerging applications of machine learning (ML) in scientific and social contexts, aiming to promote both epistemically and ethically sound practices. At present, I am a postdoctoral research associate jointly affiliated between the Center for Information Technology Policy (CITP) at Princeton University and the Natural and Artificial Minds Initiative (NAM) of the Princeton AI Lab. I am also the principal investigator on a grant from the Alfred P. Sloan Foundation assessing the epistemic credentials of AI in science evaluation.

My doctoral research developed a view of mathematical representation in which applied mathematical and computational models act as conceptual prosthetics, extending our cognitive capacities for manipulating abstract representations.

My current research examines algorithmic fairness, the epistemic reliability of ML systems, and their propensity to cause harm, while also investigating proposed uses of ML in science evaluation.
